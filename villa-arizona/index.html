Dear guest,
I am Villa Arizona.

Twenty-five minutes from the airport. Five en-suite bedrooms. The Atlantic at the bottom of the road.

I am a house in the south of Tenerife. I will not pretend that is a small thing to be, but I am going to try to be honest with you about what I am and what I am not, because most of the people who land on this page are in the wrong place, and the few who are in the right place deserve a straight conversation.

I am not a holiday rental. I am not an investment vehicle. I am not the kind of villa that exists so that someone in another country can collect a yield on it. I was built once, carefully, for a single household who wanted to actually live here. The architect knew that. The materials know that. The land I sit on knows that.
If you are looking for a property to put on a booking platform, close this page now. There is no version of this conversation in which I become that, and I would rather lose you in the first paragraph than waste your afternoon.

If you are still here, then you are probably the person I was built for. The rest of this page is for you.

Barranco del Inglés is named for an Englishman who chose this hillside in the 1970s.

The pool runs the length of the south-facing garden. The mountains do most of the talking.

Mornings here have a particular quality. The first light reaches the volcanic foothills behind me and works its way slowly down toward the sea. The pool stays still. The palms are silent. There is a hand of coffee somewhere in the house, and nowhere in particular to be.
South Tenerife averages three hundred and twenty days of sun a year. That number means less than it sounds until you have lived somewhere where February is something you survive. Here, February is a month like any other. You step outside without thinking. The stone underfoot has been warm since seven in the morning. You stop checking the forecast. After a while, you stop checking the calendar.

In November the wind drops at four in the afternoon. That is the best hour to sit by the water.

An interior courtyard with a single olive tree. A quiet place between the rooms.

I was named for a desert I do not sit in. The architect who drew me had Arizona in mind — the long horizontal lines, the way a house in that landscape refuses to compete with the land it sits on, the palette of stone and sand and first light. Low buildings. Warm materials. Rooms that open rather than enclose.
The Canary Islands are not a desert, but South Tenerife behaves like one for most of the year. The foothills behind me are volcanic. The light in July is the same light you find in Sedona at the same hour. The Sahara Noir marble in my kitchen came from the other side of an ocean that is, from here, the same ocean that washes against Africa. The palette was waiting for me before I was drawn.

A desert house on a volcanic island, built for someone who understood that the two landscapes were closer than they looked.

Inside, the boundary between me and the garden is a choice you make in the morning. Floor-to-ceiling glazing slides open from the principal living room onto the pool terrace. When it does, the dining table, the kitchen island, and the garden become one long continuous room.
Older villas here treat indoor and outdoor as separate decisions — one for sitting, one for visiting. I was built differently. The rooms exist for sleeping. Everything else happens in a single horizontal flow toward the pool. You do not host a lunch and then move it outside. You set the table, and the house rearranges itself around you.

The principal living room glazing weighs over four hundred kilograms per panel. It moves on a fingertip.

The principal living room opens completely to the garden. The sofa was placed for the view, not the television.

The kitchen is the kind of room that shapes how a household actually lives. Mine is built around a single slab of dark Sahara Noir marble — a material that does not need to compete with anything else in the room because nothing else in the room is trying to compete with it.
Dinner here is a long table for eight. A designer chandelier overhead. The doors open. A grandchild at one end, slightly too old now to sit still, slightly too young to be anywhere else. A bottle of Tenerife Listán Blanco, cold and local. The palms outside catching the last of the daylight. Someone says something that makes the whole table laugh. These are the meals people remember. Not the restaurants. Not the anniversaries at hotels. The ones that happened at home, with the doors open, with everyone in the same room for once.

The wines of South Tenerife age in volcanic soil. Listán Blanco from Vilaflor sells out by July most years.

Sahara Noir marble. German appliances. A countertop you will spend the next twenty years cooking on.

The dining chandelier was custom-fitted before the ceiling was finished. Some details only happen if planned years in advance.

The principal bedroom faces the volcanic foothills. A wall of glass. A circular reading chair in pale wool. The bed is positioned so that, in the first hour after waking, the only thing visible is the line of mountain rising into the morning sky.
The en-suite is finished in book-matched marble. Twin basins. Double mirrors with backlight warm enough to read by. The kind of room that turns the start and end of every day into something quietly intentional. Most of adult life is spent waking up in a hurry. Here, waking up is the point.

From the bed, the mountain takes up the entire window between sunrise and ten in the morning.

The principal bedroom has a single window the size of a wall. The mountain is the only artwork it needs.

Book-matched marble in the principal bathroom. Two basins, two sinks, two mirrors — built for two people who do not have to take turns.

I have five en-suite bedrooms, a basement of two hundred square metres, and a separate guest apartment with its own entrance. What this means in practice: when family arrives in July, no one has to share a bathroom. When friends come for the weekend they can stay as long as they want. When the grandchildren bring their friends one day, there is room for that too.
Most people, when they finally build the house they always wanted, build it one size too small. They optimize for today and forget that the next twenty years will include birthdays, anniversaries, Christmases, unplanned visits, the summer the children come home with someone new, the winter a friend needs somewhere quiet to recover. I was built one size larger on purpose. Six garage spaces below. A walk-in closet you do not need to apologize for. A laundry the size of someone else’s second bedroom. Enough room for the life that happens when you stop counting heads.

The basement is currently empty. It has been wired for a cinema, a gym, or both.

The entry hall has a glass elevator and a wood-and-iron staircase. A choice of two ways up.

I come furnished. The architect did not stop at the walls. Every chair, every plate, every linen, every lamp, every artwork was chosen the same way the marble was — once, by the household who built me, for the household that would actually live here next.
You will not need to buy a fork. You will not need to wait three months for the sofa. Walk in with a suitcase. The bed will be made. Most villas at this level are sold empty, so the next owner can spend eighteen months arguing with a designer about cushion fabric. I was not.

Down to the cutlery in the drawers. Down to the towels in the linen cupboard.

La Caleta started life as a fishing village, and parts of it still behave like one. Six wooden boats go out before sunrise. The seafront is a five-minute drive — octopus restaurants on the rocks, families walking the promenade in the evening, the Atlantic doing what the Atlantic does on islands that face nothing for three thousand kilometres.
The rest of the life you might want is on the same radius. Costa Adeje. The Michelin-starred kitchens of La Caleta village. The marina at Puerto Colón. The international school, if that matters. The Sky Bar at Royal Hideaway, if you forget to plan dinner. The airport is twenty-five minutes back the way you came. What La Caleta is not: a development. It was not invented for the people who will buy me. It existed first. That is why it still feels like somewhere, rather than anywhere.

Three Michelin-starred restaurants are within walking distance of my front gate.

An afternoon in shade. The villa wraps around the pool on three sides. Privacy without walls.

An outdoor dining table backed by banana plants. Everyone’s favourite room is the one without a roof.

I am not a holiday rental, and I have no interest in becoming one. I was built for a single household who wanted a real home in a country where the weather lets you live the way you have always wanted to live — and who decided that South Tenerife was the right country to do it in.
I have been here for some time. Not because no one has looked. Because the right house and the right household recognize each other, and that recognition cannot be hurried. If the photographs on this page have begun to feel less like an interesting abstraction and more like a possibility — come and walk through me. The right villa is always obvious within five minutes of the first viewing. If I am yours, you will know before you reach the kitchen.

I have been waiting.
